## 0.1 Your First 30 Days: The Action Plan

The first month is not about profit. It is about building foundations. Most new bettors lose money in their first thirty days because they bet before they understand what they are doing. You will not make this mistake. Follow this daily plan without deviation.

### Days 1-7: Observation Only

Do not place a single bet. This is non-negotiable. Your first week is dedicated to learning the landscape without financial exposure.

**Daily Tasks:**
- Open accounts at three sportsbooks (DraftKings, FanDuel, and one sharp book like Pinnacle or Circa if accessible)
- Review the daily betting slate each morning. Compare lines across your books. Note where they differ and by how much
- Read one sport-specific chapter from this book each day
- Paper trade five games daily. Record your predicted line, the market line, and which side you would bet. Do not wager real money
- Join one betting community or forum. Observe discussions without participating

**Goal:** Understand how lines move, where to find information, and what constitutes a betting opportunity. Build familiarity without financial pressure.

### Days 8-14: Micro-Stakes Practice

Begin betting with the smallest possible stakes. Most books accept bets as low as $1. This is your training ground.

**Daily Tasks:**
- Place exactly two bets per day, maximum $5 each
- One bet must be on a major market (spread or total) in a mainstream sport (NFL, NBA, MLB, NHL, or top-tier college)
- One bet must be on a market you have researched that day using the methods in Chapter 5
- Record every bet in a spreadsheet: date, sport, matchup, bet type, stake, odds, your calculated fair line, and reasoning
- Grade your bets the following day. Update your results tracker

**Goal:** Practice the mechanics of placing bets, tracking results, and building confidence. Expect to lose money or break even. This is tuition.

### Days 15-21: Building the Routine

Increase volume slightly while maintaining strict discipline. The focus shifts from mechanics to process.

**Daily Tasks:**
- Place three to five bets daily, maximum $10 each
- Implement the full morning routine from Chapter 5: overnight review, injury scan, bankroll check
- Begin calculating your own fair lines for every bet using the methods in Chapter 3
- Only bet when your calculated fair line differs from the market line by at least 2.5% in implied probability
- Review all graded bets weekly. Identify patterns in your wins and losses

**Goal:** Establish sustainable daily habits. Develop the discipline to pass on bets that do not meet your criteria.

### Days 22-30: Scaling Toward Profitability

By day twenty-two, you should have placed fifty to one hundred small bets and developed basic competency. Now you prepare for real stakes.

**Daily Tasks:**
- Increase unit size to 1% of your bankroll if results are acceptable (break-even or better)
- Maintain the same rigorous selection criteria—do not increase volume just because stakes are higher
- Begin tracking closing line value (CLV) on every bet. Compare your line to the closing line at game time
- Complete a full bankroll review. Calculate your actual edge based on CLV and results
- Draft your personal betting rules document based on mistakes made during the month

**Goal:** Prove you can follow a system. Demonstrate positive expected value through closing line analysis. Prepare for sustainable long-term operation.

**Bankroll Allocation:**

- Replenishment plan: If you lose 30% of bankroll, stop betting. Analyze mistakes. Reload only after identifying errors

## 0.3 Your First Fair Line: A Complete Walkthrough

Theory means nothing without practice. This section walks you through calculating and betting your first fair line from start to finish.

### The Setup

Date\: October 15th
Game\: Kansas City Chiefs at Denver Broncos
Market\: Chiefs -6.5 (-110) at DraftKings

You believe this line is mispriced. Here is how you prove it.

### Step 1: Power Rating

Start with team power ratings. These numerical values represent team strength. For this example:

- Kansas City Chiefs: +7.0 (elite team, approximately 7 points better than average on neutral field)
- Denver Broncos: -2.5 (below average, approximately 2.5 points worse than average)

Calculate the raw line: 7.0 - (-2.5) = 9.5 points in favor of Kansas City on a neutral field.

### Step 2: Adjustments

Apply situational factors:

**Home Field:** Denver home advantage in NFL is historically worth 2.5 points. Adjust: 9.5 - 2.5 = 7.0

**Rest:** Both teams on standard rest. No adjustment.

**Injuries:** Chiefs missing starting guard, worth approximately 0.5 points. Broncos fully healthy. Adjust: 7.0 - 0.5 = 6.5

**Weather:** Clear conditions, no adjustment needed.

**Motivation:** Both teams competitive in division, standard motivation.

Your calculated fair line: Chiefs -6.5

### Step 3: Market Comparison

DraftKings offers Chiefs -6.5 (-110). Your calculation says the fair line is Chiefs -6.5. This suggests the market price is accurate, not a betting opportunity.

But you check FanDuel: Chiefs -6 (-110). The line is one point better for the Chiefs at FanDuel.

Check Pinnacle (sharp book): Chiefs -7 (-105). The sharp book prices Kansas City as one-point bigger favorites.

### Step 4: The Bet Decision

Pinnacle at -7 suggests your -6.5 calculation might be slightly conservative. The true fair line likely lies between -6.5 and -7.0. DraftKings at -6.5 offers fair value. FanDuel at -6 offers clear value—you are getting a full point better than your fair line.

**The Bet:** Chiefs -6 at FanDuel, risking 1 unit ($10 on $1,000 bankroll)

**Why:** Your fair line is Chiefs -6.5 to -7.0. You are getting -6. That half-point difference represents approximately 2.8% in expected value. Over hundreds of similar bets, this edge compounds into profit.

### Step 5: Record and Review

Log the bet:
- Date: October 15
- Sport: NFL
- Matchup: Chiefs at Broncos
- Bet: Chiefs -6 (-110)
- Stake: $10
- Fair Line: Chiefs -6.5
- Edge: 0.5 points (approximately 2.8% EV)
- Closing Line: (fill in after game)
- Result: (fill in after game)

After the game, grade the result and update your tracker. Whether you win or lose, the process was correct. That is what matters.

## 0.4 Common Beginner Mistakes: What Not to Do

The market separates winners from losers quickly. Avoid these fatal errors that destroy new bettors within their first month.

### Mistake 1: Betting Without a Fair Line

Never bet because you "think" a team will win. Never bet because you "have a feeling." Calculate a fair line for every bet. If you cannot justify the number, you cannot justify the bet. Intuition is not an edge. Math is.

### Mistake 2: Chasing Losses

You will lose bets. Sometimes three in a row. Sometimes five. Increasing stakes to "make it back" guarantees bankruptcy. Your unit size is fixed. It does not change because you are down. It does not change because you are up. Consistency is survival.

### Mistake 3: Parlays and Exotic Bets

Parlays, teasers, and same-game parlays are designed to extract maximum juice from recreational bettors. The math is brutal. A two-team parlay at -110 each has an expected value of approximately -9% versus -4.5% for straight bets. Avoid these entirely for your first six months.

### Mistake 4: Betting Your Favorite Team

Emotional attachment destroys objectivity. You cannot fairly assess the team you grew up watching. You see their potential, not their performance. You remember the comeback wins, not the blowout losses. Do not bet on teams you care about. Period.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Bankroll Management

Betting 5% of your bankroll on a "lock" is not confidence. It is arrogance. Even bets with 60% win probability lose 40% of the time. Variance is real and it is brutal. Bet 1% per wager. Maximum 3% daily exposure. No exceptions.

### Mistake 6: Failing to Track Results

You cannot improve what you do not measure. Every bet goes in the spreadsheet. Every day. No exceptions. After one hundred bets, patterns emerge. You discover which sports you handicap well. You identify which bet types destroy your bankroll. Data drives improvement. Memory lies.

### Mistake 7: Overconfidence After Early Wins

Beginner's luck is dangerous. Winning your first five bets does not mean you have talent. It means variance smiled on you. Stay humble. Stick to the process. The market will humble you eventually—it is only a question of whether you survive the lesson.

You have read the chapters. You have set up your accounts. You understand fair lines and bankroll management. When do you actually start?

Place your first real bet when you meet ALL of these criteria:

**You Have a Valid Fair Line Calculation**
You can explain exactly why the market line is wrong. You have adjusted for injuries, rest, weather, and motivation. Your number is defensible, not guessed.

**You Have Line Shopped**
You have checked at least three sportsbooks. You are taking the best available price. You are not accepting -115 when -110 exists elsewhere. You are not betting into markets with significant juice.

**The Edge Exceeds Your Threshold**
Your calculated fair line differs from the market line by at least 2.5% in implied probability. For spreads, this typically means at least a half-point of value. Anything less and variance will consume your edge.

**Your Bankroll is Properly Sized**
You have $1,000 or more in dedicated betting funds. Your bet size is 1% of that bankroll ($10). You are prepared to lose this money without affecting your life.

**You Are Emotionally Neutral**
You are not betting to recover losses. You are not betting because you are bored. You are not betting because your friend gave you a tip. You are betting because the math demands it.

**You Have Time to Track It**
You will record this bet in your spreadsheet immediately after placing it. You will grade the result when it concludes. You will review the closing line value. You are committing to the full process, not just the wager.

# Chapter 1: The Hold: Understanding Vigorish
The Hold
Every sportsbook is a store. The product is probability. The price is odds. And like any store, the house takes a
cut.
That cut is called the hold. Some call it the vig, the juice, the margin, or the overround. The name does not matter.
What matters is that it exists on nearly every bet you place, and it is the reason you must win more than half your
wagers just to break even.
If you do not understand the hold, nothing else in this book will help you. Not the bankroll rules. Not the edge
calculations. Not the psychology chapters.
The hold is the mountain you climb before the race even starts.
Odds Are a Language
American odds tell a story in two dialects: negative and positive.
Negative odds — the minus sign — tell you how much you must risk to win one hundred dollars. A line of -150
means you lay down one hundred fifty dollars to win one hundred. The team is favored. The outcome is
considered likely.
Positive odds — the plus sign — tell you how much you win if you risk one hundred dollars. A line of
+200 means you lay down one hundred to win two hundred. The team is the underdog. The outcome is
considered less likely.
Most bettors stop here. They see the numbers as instructions for payout. But beneath every set of odds is a
hidden percentage: the implied probability. The sportsbook is telling you, in its own language, how likely it thinks
an outcome is. Your job is to learn that language, translate it, and then argue with it.
Converting Odds to Implied
Probability
The formulas are simple. Do not let the notation intimidate you.
For negative odds (favorites):
Implied Probability = |Odds| / (|Odds| + 100)
Example: -150 > 150 / (150 + 100) = 150 / 250 =
0.60 = 60%
For positive odds (underdogs):
Implied Probability = 100 / (Odds + 100)
Example: +200 > 100 / (200 + 100) = 100 / 300 =
0.333 = 33.3%
A quick sanity check: lower implied probability
means a bigger payout, because the event is considered less likely. Higher implied probability means a smaller
payout, because the event is considered more likely. If your conversion gives you the opposite relationship, you
have done the math wrong.

Reducing the Hold
You cannot eliminate the hold, but you can shrink it. Three methods:
Line shopping. Different books offer different
prices on the same event. Having accounts at three or more sportsbooks is not optional for a serious bettor.
It is the cost of admission. A half-point on a spread or five cents of vig can be the difference between a profitable
year and a losing one. Over one thousand bets, the difference between -110 and -115 is thousands of dollars.
Reduced-juice books. Some books offer -105 on standard bets instead of -110. The hold drops from roughly 4.7%
to roughly 2.4%. If you can access these books, use them. The savings are not theoretical.
They are compound interest on every winning bet.
Exchange betting. Betting exchanges like Betfair match bettors against each other rather than against the house.
The exchange takes a commission on winnings, typically 2% to 5%, but the implied odds are often closer to true
probability because there is no built-in hold on the price itself. The trade-off is liquidity: some markets are thin, and
you may not get your full stake matched.
The Bottom Line
The hold is not your enemy in the sense that a sportsbook is not your enemy. The sportsbook is a tollbooth. It will
let you pass, but it will charge you every time. Your job is to bring enough edge to pay the toll and still have profit
left over.
If you cannot convert odds to implied probability in under ten seconds, practice until you can. This is not optional
arithmetic. It is the language of the market.
And you cannot argue with a market you cannot understand.

#### Vig Math Walkthrough: Finding the True Breakeven
The breakeven point is the win percentage you must hit for a bet to produce zero profit and zero loss over the
long run. It is not 50%. At standard -110 odds, it is significantly higher.
For negative American odds (the amount you must risk to win $100):
Breakeven % = |Risk| / (|Risk| + 100)
At -110, you risk $110 to win $100:
110 / (110 + 100) = 110 / 210 = 0.5238 = 52.38%
You must win 52.38% of your -110 bets just to break even. Every win below that rate bleeds money.
At -105:
105 / (105 + 100) = 105 / 205 = 0.5122 = 51.22%
At -200:
200 / (200 + 100) = 200 / 300 = 0.6667 = 66.67%
For positive American odds (the amount you win on a $100 risk):
Breakeven % = 100 / (100 + Win)
At +100 (even money):
100 / (100 + 100) = 100 / 200 = 0.5000 = 50.00%
At +150:
100 / (100 + 150) = 100 / 250 = 0.4000 = 40.00%
At +125:
100 / (100 + 125) = 100 / 225 = 0.4444 = 44.44%
Notice the asymmetry: as a favorite gets heavier (more negative), the breakeven climbs fast. As an underdog gets
longer (more positive), the breakeven drops, but you still need to hit that lower rate consistently.

#### Why Reduced Juice (-105) Is Worth Changing Sportsbooks For
Bettors obsess over finding the "right side" of a game. They should obsess over the price they pay to play it.
At -110, you pay a 4.76% hold. At -105, you pay a 2.38% hold. The house edge is cut in half.
If you bet $100 per game, the difference per wager is small in absolute dollars: roughly $2.38 less in expected vig
at -105 versus -110. But absolute dollars are the wrong lens. The correct lens is *edge required to profit*.
To beat -110, you must find +2.38% edge over the true probability (52.38% breakeven vs. 50% fair).
To beat -105, you must find only +1.22% edge (51.22% breakeven vs. 50% fair).
Finding an extra 1.16% edge per game is extraordinarily difficult. It often requires moving an entire half-point on a
spread or knowing injury news before the market adjusts. Meanwhile, logging into a -105 book requires only a
different username and password.
If your current book offers -110 and a competitor offers -105 on the same market, the competitor is giving you a
1.16% head start on every single bet. Over a season, that is more valuable than almost any handicapping insight
you will develop in your first year.
Do not be loyal to a sportsbook. Be loyal to the price.

# Chapter 2: The Void: When Not to Bet
Expected Value
Understanding the hold tells you what you are paying. Understanding expected value tells you whether you
should buy at all.
Expected value — EV — is the average amount you can expect to win or lose on a bet if you placed it thousands
of times. It is the mathematical truth beneath the result. A bet can lose and still have positive expected value. A
bet can win and still have negative expected value. The outcome of a single wager is noise. The expected value
is signal.
If you internalize only one concept from this book, make it this one.

### The EV Formula in Plain English
Before we dissect real markets, nail down the intuition. Expected value is the average outcome if you could repeat
a bet thousands of times.
EV = (Probability of Win × Net Win) − (Probability of Loss × Net Loss)
"Net Win" is what you actually collect *above* your stake. A +150 bet returns $250 total on a $100 risk, so Net
Win = $150. A −110 bet returns $190.91 total, so Net Win = $90.91. "Net Loss" is almost always your stake
itself—what you forfeit when the bet misses.
Think of EV as a weighted scale. You place the profit you expect to gain on one side, multiplied by how often it
happens, and the loss you expect to suffer on the other, multiplied by how often *that* happens. When the profit
side is heavier, the bet is +EV. When the loss side is heavier, you are paying a toll to the sportsbook.

### Implied Probability vs. True Probability
Sportsbooks do not tell you the probability they believe an event holds. They tell you a price, and from that price
you must reverse-engineer an *implied probability*—the breakeven rate required for a bet to return zero EV at
that line.
For positive American odds (+):
Implied Probability = 100 / (Odds + 100)
Example: +150 → 100 / 250 = 40.0%

### The Law of Large Numbers
EV is a theoretical average. It only manifests through volume. Flip a weighted coin that lands heads 55% of the
time. Over 10 flips, you might see 3 heads and 7 tails. Over 100 flips, variance still stings. Over 1,000 flips, the
ratio converges toward 55%. Over 10,000 flips, the edge is nearly guaranteed to appear in your results.
Sports betting is no different. A +5% EV edge is invisible over a weekend. It is meaningful over a month. It is
dominant over a season. Your bankroll will swing—drawdowns of 10, 20, even 30 units are possible with perfectly
sound edges—but the math does not lie. Volume is the bridge between theory and profit.
This is why unit discipline matters. If you size bets based on emotion after a losing streak, you shrink your volume
at the exact moment variance is hurting you. Consistent stake sizing keeps you in the game long enough for the
law of large numbers to rescue your bottom line.

# Chapter 6: Bankroll Management
## The One-Percent Rule and Eliote Reserve System

> "Your bankroll is your business capital. Protect it like a Turtle protects its shell."

This chapter is not about maximizing growth. It is about surviving long enough for growth to happen. A bettor with a 55% win rate and a 10% unit size has a high probability of ruin. A bettor with a 52% win rate and a 1% unit size has a low probability of ruin. Edge is not enough. Survival is the prerequisite.

---

## The One-Percent Rule

**Definition:** One unit equals exactly 1% of your current bankroll.

**Example:**
- Bankroll: $5,000
- Unit size: $50 (1% of $5,000)
- Bet: 1 unit = $50

**Why 1%?**

Variance is cruel. Even with a 55% win rate (excellent by any standard), you will experience losing streaks. The 1% rule ensures you survive them.

**The Math of Ruin:**

| Unit Size | 10-Loss Streak Impact | Recovery Required |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1% | -9.6% of bankroll | +10.6% to recover |
| 2% | -18.3% of bankroll | +22.4% to recover |
| 5% | -40.1% of bankroll | +67.0% to recover |
| 10% | -65.1% of bankroll | +186.7% to recover |

A 10-loss streak is not hypothetical. If your true win rate is 55%, you have approximately a 5% chance of hitting a 10-loss streak over 500 wagers. That is 1 in 20 betting careers. The 1% rule ensures you are still in the game when it happens.

**The 1% Rule Is Non-Negotiable:**
- Never increase unit size because you are "running hot."
- Never decrease unit size because you are "due for a loss."
- Resize only by the math (weekly bankroll updates), never by the mood.

## The Kelly Criterion (Advanced)

The Kelly Criterion is a formula for optimal bet sizing given your edge:

```
Kelly % = (bp - q) / b

Where:
b = decimal odds - 1 (net return per unit)
p = your estimated win probability
q = 1 - p (loss probability)
```

**Example:**
- Bet: +100 (even money)
- Your win probability: 55%
- b = 1.0 (decimal 2.0 - 1)
- p = 0.55
- q = 0.45

```
Kelly % = (1.0 × 0.55 - 0.45) / 1.0
Kelly % = (0.55 - 0.45) / 1.0
Kelly % = 0.10 = 10%
```

**Full Kelly is 10% of bankroll.** This is aggressive.

### Why We Use Fractional Kelly

| Problem | Full Kelly Risk | Fractional Kelly Solution |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Edge estimation error | If you think edge is 5% but it's 2%, full Kelly destroys you | Half-Kelly reduces impact of overconfidence |
| Variance drag | 50% drawdowns are statistically expected | Quarter-Kelly reduces variance by ~87% |
| Multiple simultaneous bets | Full Kelly assumes one bet at a time | Fractional Kelly accounts for correlation risk |

### Recommended Fractional Kelly

| Experience Level | Kelly Fraction | Unit Size |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------|
| Beginner (<100 bets) | Do not use Kelly | 1% flat |
| Intermediate (100-500 bets) | Quarter-Kelly | 25% of Kelly calc |
| Advanced (500+ bets, verified edge) | Half-Kelly | 50% of Kelly calc |
| Professional (1000+ bets, 3%+ ROI) | Full Kelly (optional) | 100% of Kelly calc |

**For 95% of readers:** Stick with 1% flat. Kelly is a theoretical benchmark, not a practical tool for most bettors.

## Bankroll Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Hot Streak

**Situation:** You are up 25% in 3 weeks.

**Temptation:** Increase unit size to 2% or 3%. "I've proven myself."

**Correct Action:** Maintain 1% units. Resize only based on bankroll, not confidence.

**Why:** Variance works both ways. A 25% gain can become a 10% loss in one bad week if you increase stakes.

---

### Scenario 2: The Cold Streak

**Situation:** You are down 15% in 2 weeks.

**Temptation:** Decrease unit size to 0.5%. "I'll bet small until my luck turns."

**Correct Action:** Maintain 1% units. Your edge has not changed. Variance is noise.

**Why:** Reducing stakes during a downswing locks in underperformance. The math says stay the course.

---

### Scenario 3: The Replenishment Decision

**Situation:** You are down 22%. Your 7-day break is complete. Your audit shows process is sound.

**Decision:** Replenish from reserve or not?

**Framework:**
- If you have identified NO systematic errors: Replenish.
- If you have identified FIXABLE errors: Fix first, then replenish.
- If you have identified FUNDAMENTAL errors (model is broken): Do not replenish. Rebuild model first.
